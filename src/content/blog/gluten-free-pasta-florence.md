Here's the counterintuitive truth every celiac traveller learns on arrival: **Italy, the country of pasta, is one of the easiest places in Europe to eat gluten-free.** Celiac awareness here is decades ahead of most countries — screening is common, the national celiac association (AIC) certifies restaurants, and every waiter in a tourist city has heard *"senza glutine"* a thousand times. Florence is no exception.

## The three phrases that do the work

- **"Senza glutine"** (SEN-za GLOO-tee-neh) — without gluten. The label to look for on menus and packaging.
- **"Sono celiaco / celiaca"** (m/f) — *I am celiac.* This is the important one: it tells the kitchen you're not dieting, you're medical. Italian kitchens take it seriously.
- **"C'è rischio di contaminazione?"** — *Is there a risk of cross-contamination?* Use it anywhere fresh pasta is made in-house, because flour travels.

Saying *sono celiaco* changes the conversation from "we have a GF option" to "let me check how we'd cook it" — which is the conversation you want.

## Eating out in Florence, realistically

**The good news:** risotto, grilled meats and fish, most beans and vegetable sides, and Tuscany's bean soups (when not thickened with bread) are naturally gluten-free — the traditional Florentine table has plenty for you beyond pasta. Dedicated GF pasta (usually rice- or corn-based) is increasingly common in trattorias.

**The caution:** the two Tuscan classics to watch are **ribollita and pappa al pomodoro — both are bread soups**, and fresh-pasta kitchens are, by definition, full of airborne flour. In a trattoria that hand-rolls its pasta each morning, ask the contamination question even if GF pasta is on the menu. Gelato is mostly safe, but ask about cones and cross-contaminated scoops.

**The shortcut:** the AIC (Associazione Italiana Celiachia) maintains a list of certified restaurants; searching "AIC Firenze" before you travel gives you a vetted map of the city.

## Can you take a pasta making class if you're gluten-free?

Yes — with the right setup, and this is worth checking before you book *any* class, including ours. The honest requirements are:

1. **A dedicated gluten-free flour blend**, not "the same recipe with different flour" improvised on the day.
2. **A clean, separate station** — fresh pasta classes are floury places, and a wiped-down corner of a shared table isn't enough for a celiac guest.
3. **Advance notice**, so the kitchen sets up before the flour starts flying.

In [our classes](/pasta-making-class-florence/), we prepare a dedicated gluten-free blend and a clean station **at no extra charge** — you just tell us about any allergy or intolerance when you book. You roll, shape and eat alongside everyone else at the table; the only difference is the flour. The same applies to [private groups](/private-cooking-class-florence/), where mixed tables (one celiac guest among eight) are completely routine for us.

## The bottom line

Don't let gluten decide your Florence trip. Learn the two sentences, ask the contamination question in fresh-pasta kitchens, favour AIC-listed spots when you want zero-effort dinners — and if making pasta with your own hands is on the list, it doesn't have to come off.
